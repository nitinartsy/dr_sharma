KIDNEY CANCER • ROBOTIC SURGERY

Patient Guide: Robotic Partial Nephrectomy (RAPN) — Saving the Kidney

By Dr. Gopal Sharma, MCh MRCS • Manipal Hospital, Dwarka

Robotic Partial Nephrectomy is a minimally invasive surgery to remove a renal tumor (mass) while preserving the rest of the kidney. It is the preferred treatment for small-to-medium kidney tumors because saving kidney tissue lowers your risk of developing chronic kidney disease or needing dialysis in the future.

Part 1: The Surgical Steps

The surgery is performed using the da Vinci Surgical System and typically takes 2 to 4 hours.

  • 1
    Positioning & Access

    You will be placed on your side (flank position) to give the surgeon access to the kidney. 4–5 small “keyhole” incisions (8–12mm) are made on the abdomen to insert the robotic camera and instruments.

  • 2
    Exposing the Kidney

    The kidney sits behind the intestines. The surgeon carefully moves the colon (bowel) aside to reveal the kidney. Hilar Dissection: The surgeon locates the main renal artery and vein — critical to temporarily stop blood flow to cut out the tumor safely.

  • 3
    Defining the Tumor (Ultrasound)

    A specialized robotic ultrasound probe is placed directly on the kidney to help the surgeon “see” inside the organ and mark exactly where the tumor ends and healthy tissue begins.

  • 4
    Clamping — Warm Ischemia

    Small clamps are placed on the kidney artery to stop blood flow. The robot’s speed and precision allow us to keep this time short (usually under 20–25 minutes) to prevent kidney damage.

  • 5
    Tumor Excision

    Using robotic scissors, the tumor is cut out with a thin rim of healthy tissue (margin) to ensure no cancer cells are left behind.

  • 6
    Reconstruction (Renorrhaphy)

    The surgeon sews the kidney back together in two layers: Deep Layer to close the urinary collecting system and stop bleeding; Outer Layer to pull kidney edges together (like closing a clam). Blood flow is then restored.

  • 7
    Removal

    The tumor is placed in a bag and removed through one of the small incisions.

Part 2: Potential Complications & Risks

Bleeding & Transfusion

The kidney filters 20% of the body’s blood. While robotic tools minimize blood loss, there is a risk. Occasionally (<5%) a blood transfusion is needed.

Conversion to Total Removal

If the tumor is too close to major vessels or bleeding heavily, the surgeon may remove the entire kidney (<5% of cases) for your safety.

Urine Leak (Urinoma)

A small drain tube is left after surgery. If a leak occurs, it usually heals on its own. Occasionally a ureteral stent may be needed.

General Surgical Risks

Infection at incision sites or urinary tract; hernia (rare with small cuts); ileus (temporary bowel slowing causing bloating).

KIDNEY CANCER • ROBOTIC SURGERY

Robotic Radical Nephrectomy (RRN) — Complete Kidney Removal with Minimal Invasion

By Dr. Gopal Sharma, MCh MRCS • Manipal Hospital, Dwarka

Robotic Radical Nephrectomy is a minimally invasive surgery to remove the entire kidney, the surrounding fat, and often the adrenal gland. It is primarily performed to treat kidney cancer (Renal Cell Carcinoma). Using the da Vinci Surgical System, this procedure is performed through small “keyhole” incisions rather than the large flank incision used in traditional open surgery.

Part 1: The Surgical Steps

The surgery typically takes 2 to 3 hours and is performed under general anesthesia.

  • 1
    Positioning & Access

    You will be placed on your side (flank position) to expose the kidney area. The abdomen is inflated with carbon dioxide gas to create working space. The surgeon makes 3–4 small incisions (ports) for the robotic arms and one slightly larger incision (usually hidden in the lower abdomen or bikini line) to eventually remove the kidney.

  • 2
    Mobilizing the Organs

    The kidney sits behind the intestines. The surgeon gently moves the colon (bowel) away. Right Kidney: The liver is gently lifted up. Left Kidney: The spleen and pancreas are carefully separated from the kidney.

  • 3
    Controlling the Blood Supply (Critical Step)

    The surgeon identifies the Renal Artery (which brings blood to the kidney) and the Renal Vein. These major blood vessels are individually sealed and divided using titanium staples or Hem-o-lok clips to prevent bleeding.

  • 4
    Removing the Kidney

    Once the blood supply is cut, the surgeon dissects the kidney free from its surrounding attachments and the ureter. The entire kidney (enclosed in a protective bag) is removed through the extraction incision.

Part 2: Potential Risks & Complications

Bleeding

Although less common than in open surgery, significant bleeding can occur if a staple malfunctions or a vessel is injured. Transfusion is rarely needed (<5%).

Injury to Surrounding Organs

Bowel injury (rare). Minor scratches to spleen/liver can happen during retraction but usually stop bleeding on their own.

Ileus (Slow Bowels)

The intestines may temporarily “go to sleep” after surgery, causing bloating, nausea, and constipation for a few days.

Pneumothorax

Because the kidney sits close to the diaphragm, a small hole can sometimes form in the lining of the lung. Usually resolves on its own or requires a small tube for a day.

Living with One Kidney

Most people live a completely normal life with one healthy kidney. However, you will have a slightly higher risk of developing high blood pressure or Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) later.

Incisional Hernia

There is a small risk that tissue can bulge through the extraction incision site.

PROSTATE CANCER • ROBOTIC SURGERY

Patient Guide: Robotic Radical Prostatectomy (RARP) — Understanding the Procedure & Risks

By Dr. Gopal Sharma, MCh MRCS • Manipal Hospital, Dwarka

Robotic Radical Prostatectomy is a minimally invasive surgery to remove the entire prostate gland and seminal vesicles. It is the most common treatment for localized prostate cancer. The surgery typically takes 2 to 3 hours, performed using the da Vinci Surgical System.

Part 1: The Surgical Steps

  • 1
    Access & Port Placement

    The abdomen is inflated with carbon dioxide gas to create working space. 5–6 small “keyhole” incisions (8–12mm) are made across the lower abdomen. The robotic camera and instrument arms are inserted through these ports.

  • 2
    Dropping the Bladder

    The bladder is carefully detached to create a space (the “Retzius space”) to access the prostate, which sits deep in the pelvis.

  • 3
    Dissecting the Prostate

    The Endopelvic Fascia (tissues covering the prostate sides) are opened. The Dorsal Vein Complex (a cluster of large veins on top of the prostate) is carefully controlled. The prostate is then separated from the bladder at the Bladder Neck, carefully preserving the bladder muscle for future urinary control.

  • 4
    Nerve Sparing (Critical Step)

    The neurovascular bundles (nerves responsible for erections) run along the sides of the prostate. The surgeon carefully peels these nerves away from the prostate — done gently without using heat (cautery) whenever possible to avoid nerve damage. Note: If cancer is very close to the nerves, they may need to be removed.

  • 5
    Removing the Prostate & Seminal Vesicles

    The prostate is separated from the urethra at the apex. The seminal vesicles (glands behind the prostate) are also removed. The entire specimen is placed in a small plastic bag inside the abdomen for later removal.

  • 6
    Lymph Node Dissection

    If your cancer is intermediate or high risk, the surgeon will remove lymph nodes from the pelvic area to check if the cancer has spread.

  • 7
    Reconstruction (The Anastomosis)

    The bladder is reconnected to the urethra using a continuous stitch. A Catheter (Foley) is placed through the penis into the bladder to drain urine and allow this new connection to heal.

Part 2: Potential Complications & Risks

A. Early Complications (0–30 Days)

Bleeding

Significant blood loss is rare in robotic surgery (<5% risk), but a transfusion may be required.

Infection

Risk of wound infection or urinary tract infection (UTI). Antibiotics are given to prevent this.

Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Compression stockings and sometimes blood thinners are used to prevent blood clots in the leg.

Lymphocele

If lymph nodes were removed, fluid can collect in the pelvis. Usually reabsorbed by the body; sometimes needs draining.

B. Long-Term Functional Complications

Urinary Incontinence

Almost every patient has some leakage after catheter removal. Most men regain control within 3–6 months using Kegel exercises. 90–95% become dry long-term.

Erectile Dysfunction

Temporary ED is common as nerves are “stunned” even if spared. Erections can take 12–24 months to return. Penile rehabilitation (daily Cialis/Viagra or vacuum pumps) helps recovery.

Penile Shortening

Some men notice a slight loss of penile length (usually 1–2 cm) after surgery because a section of the prostatic urethra is removed.

Inguinal Hernia

There is a slightly increased risk of developing a hernia in the groin area in the years following this surgery.

BLADDER CANCER • ROBOTIC SURGERY

Robotic Radical Cystectomy with Ileal Conduit (RARC) — Complete Patient Guide

By Dr. Gopal Sharma, MCh MRCS • Manipal Hospital, Dwarka

Robotic Radical Cystectomy is a minimally invasive procedure to remove the urinary bladder (and nearby lymph nodes/organs) to treat bladder cancer. Because the bladder is removed, the surgeon creates a new way for urine to leave your body using a small piece of your intestine, called an Ileal Conduit. Using the da Vinci Surgical System, the surgeon controls robotic arms with high precision through small “keyhole” incisions.

Part 1: The Surgical Procedure (Step-by-Step)

The surgery typically takes 4 to 6 hours and is performed under general anesthesia.

  • 1
    Access & Preparation

    The abdomen is gently inflated with gas (carbon dioxide) to create space. Instead of one large incision, 5–6 small incisions (less than 1 inch) are made — the robotic camera and instruments are inserted through these “ports.”

  • 2
    Removal (The Cystectomy)

    The surgeon carefully separates the bladder from surrounding structures. For Men: The prostate and seminal vesicles are usually removed. For Women: The uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries, and part of the vagina may be removed. Lymph nodes from the pelvic area are removed and sent to the lab to check for spread. The bladder and organs are removed through one slightly larger incision (usually near the belly button).

  • 3
    Reconstruction (The Ileal Conduit)

    Isolation: The surgeon selects a short segment (about 6 inches) of the small intestine (ileum). This piece is disconnected from the rest of the bowel, which is then reconnected so digestion continues normally. Connection: The tubes from your kidneys (ureters) are sewn into one end of this intestine segment. Stoma Creation: The other end is brought out through an opening in the abdominal wall to form a stoma. Small plastic stents are placed inside the ureters temporarily to help the connection heal.

Part 2: Potential Issues & Complications

Early Complications (0–30 Days)

Bowel ileus, wound infection, urinary leak at the anastomosis, blood clots (DVT/PE), and bleeding requiring transfusion. These are managed closely by the surgical team.

Late Complications (Long-Term)

Stomal complications, uretero-ileal stricture (narrowing at the kidney-to-bowel connection), metabolic changes due to the intestinal segment, and pelvic floor changes in women.

TESTICULAR CANCER • ROBOTIC SURGERY

Patient Guide: Robotic Retroperitoneal Lymph Node Dissection (R-RPLND) for Testicular Cancer

By Dr. Gopal Sharma, MCh MRCS • Manipal Hospital, Dwarka

Robotic RPLND is a specialized, minimally invasive surgery used to remove lymph nodes from the back of the abdomen (retroperitoneum). It is primarily used to treat testicular cancer. The surgery typically takes 3 to 5 hours and is performed under general anesthesia.

Part 1: The Surgical Steps (The Procedure)

  • 1
    Positioning & Access

    You will be placed on your side (flank position) to allow gravity to help move the bowel out of the way naturally. The surgeon makes 4–6 small “keyhole” incisions (8–12mm) on your abdomen. The da Vinci Robot is docked in place and the surgeon sits at a console to control the robotic arms with high precision.

  • 2
    Exposure (Creating the Workspace)

    The surgeon carefully lifts the colon and small intestine to the side. This reveals the Retroperitoneum — the area deep in the back where the major blood vessels (Aorta and Vena Cava) and the target lymph nodes are located.

  • 3
    “Split and Roll” Technique

    The lymph nodes in this area are wrapped tightly around the major blood vessels. The surgeon carefully splits the tissue over the vessels and “rolls” the lymph nodes off the artery and vein. This requires extreme precision to avoid injuring these major blood vessels.

  • 4
    Nerve Sparing (Critical for Function)

    Tiny nerves responsible for ejaculation (sympathetic nerve chains) run parallel to the spine and blood vessels. Using the robot’s magnified 3D view, the surgeon identifies these nerves and carefully separates them from the lymph node packets. The Goal: To preserve the ability to ejaculate normally (antegrade ejaculation).

  • 5
    Removal & Closure

    The lymph nodes are placed in a retrieval bag and removed through one of the small incisions. Lymphatic Control: The surgeon uses special clips or sealers to close the cut ends of the lymph vessels to prevent fluid leakage. The bowel is placed back in its normal position and the skin incisions are closed with dissolvable stitches and glue.

Part 2: Potential Issues & Complications

A. Specific Complications to RPLND

Retrograde Ejaculation (“Dry Orgasm”)

If nerves controlling the bladder neck are damaged, semen may flow backward into the bladder during orgasm. Not harmful to health but affects natural fertility. With nerve-sparing techniques, this risk is significantly reduced.

Chylous Ascites (Lymph Fluid Leak)

A milky fluid called “chyle” can sometimes leak from the area where nodes were removed. You will likely be placed on a Low-Fat Diet for 2–4 weeks after surgery to allow the vessels to seal.

Ureteral Injury

The ureters (tubes carrying urine from kidney to bladder) run through the surgical field. While rare, there is a small risk of injury requiring repair or a temporary stent.

General Surgical Risks

Bleeding (the robotic view makes this risk lower than open surgery), Bowel Ileus (walking helps resolve this), and Infection (managed with antibiotics).
